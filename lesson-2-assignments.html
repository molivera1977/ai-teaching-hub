📌 What You'll Be Able to Do

By the end of this lesson you'll know how to use AI to create:

  • Multiple choice and short-answer quizzes
  • Reading passages on any topic at any grade level
  • Grading rubrics for essays and projects
  • Differentiated versions of the same assignment (easier, harder, or adapted for English learners)
  • Discussion questions, exit tickets, and warm-ups
💡 Remember from Lesson 1: The better your prompt, the better your result. Each section below includes ready-made prompts you can copy and paste directly into Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, or Copilot.
Step 2 of 6 — Creating Quizzes

✅ Part 1 — Creating Quizzes

AI can write a full quiz in seconds. Just tell it the subject, grade, format, and number of questions. Here are prompts you can copy and use right now:

📋 Multiple Choice Quiz  
Create a 10-question multiple choice quiz about [TOPIC] for [GRADE] students. Each question should have 4 answer choices. Include an answer key at the end.
📋 Short Answer Quiz  
Write a 5-question short-answer quiz about [TOPIC] for [GRADE] students. Questions should require students to explain their thinking, not just recall facts. Include a sample answer for each question.
📋 True/False + Correction  
Create a 10-question true/false quiz about [TOPIC] for [GRADE]. For every false statement, students must rewrite it to make it true. Include an answer key.
🧪 Your Turn

Paste one of these prompts into Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, or Copilot. Replace [TOPIC] and [GRADE] with your own. Share the result with a colleague!

Step 3 of 6 — Writing Reading Passages

📖 Part 2 — Writing Reading Passages

Need a text about a specific science or social studies topic? AI can write one matched to your exact grade level and length. This is one of the biggest time-savers for teachers.

📋 Informational Passage  
Write an informational reading passage about [TOPIC] for [GRADE] students. The passage should be approximately [LENGTH] words. Use clear, engaging language appropriate for this grade level. Include a title and 3–5 comprehension questions at the end.
📋 Narrative / Story  
Write a short fictional story for [GRADE] students that teaches the concept of [TOPIC/THEME]. The story should be about [LENGTH] words and include relatable characters. Add 3 discussion questions at the end.
📋 Two-Sided Debate Passage  
Write a two-sided informational passage about [TOPIC] for [GRADE] students. Present one paragraph arguing for one side and one paragraph for the other side. Then add 2 questions asking students to choose a side and support it with evidence from the text.
✅ Pro tip — reading levels: You can ask AI to match a specific reading level. The simplest way: just say "beginner," "on grade level," or "advanced" and AI will adjust automatically. If your school uses Lexile scores (a numbered system for measuring reading difficulty — for example, 600L is roughly 4th grade level and 970L is roughly 7th grade level), you can be even more precise: "Write this at approximately a 600L Lexile level." Either way works.
Step 4 of 6 — Creating Rubrics

📊 Part 3 — Creating Rubrics

Rubrics used to take forever to build from scratch. AI can produce a complete, detailed rubric in about 30 seconds. You can then tweak it to match your exact expectations.

📋 Essay Rubric  
Create a 4-point rubric for grading a [GRADE] opinion essay. Include the following categories: Claim/Thesis, Evidence & Support, Organization, and Language/Conventions. Format it as a table with clear descriptions for each score level (4=Exceeds, 3=Meets, 2=Approaching, 1=Beginning).
📋 Project Rubric  
Create a rubric for a [GRADE] student project about [TOPIC]. The project involves [DESCRIBE THE PROJECT]. Include 4 categories that are most important for this assignment. Use a 1–4 scale with clear descriptions at each level.
📋 Presentation Rubric  
Write a rubric for evaluating a student oral presentation for [GRADE]. Include categories for: Content Knowledge, Organization, Speaking Clearly, and Visual Aids. Use a 4-point scale and student-friendly language so students can self-assess.
Step 5 of 6 — Differentiating Assignments

🎯 Part 4 — Differentiating Assignments

This is where AI truly shines for teachers. You can take any assignment and instantly create easier or harder versions — or adapt it for English learners or students with reading challenges.

How it works — step by step:

Step 1 — Open your assignment on your computer

Open the Word doc, Google Doc, or wherever your assignment lives. You need to be able to see the text on your screen before you can copy it.

📋 Steps 2–4 use copy and paste. If you haven't done the Start Here lesson yet, go there first — it walks through copy and paste from scratch with a practice exercise. It takes about 5 minutes and will make the steps below feel easy.

Already comfortable with copy and paste? Here's the quick version:
  • Step 2 — Highlight: Click and drag your mouse over the text you want. The selected text turns blue. (Shortcut: Ctrl+A on Windows or Cmd+A on Mac selects everything at once.)
  • Step 3 — Copy: Right-click the highlighted text and choose Copy. Nothing visible happens — your computer is now holding the text, ready to place it. (Shortcut: Ctrl+C on Windows or Cmd+C on Mac.)
  • Step 4 — Paste: Open Claude or ChatGPT, click inside the typing box, then right-click and choose Paste. Your assignment text appears. (Shortcut: Ctrl+V on Windows or Cmd+V on Mac.)
📎 Have something already saved on your computer? Skip the copy-paste entirely. Click the 📎 paperclip icon inside the typing box and a window will open showing your computer's files. Find your document — a Word file, a PDF, a saved quiz — double-click it, and AI will read it for you. No typing, no copy-pasting needed.

📱 Have a printed worksheet you want to photograph? That's a great time-saver too — but it involves a skill we haven't covered yet. Lesson 3 walks you through exactly how to take a phone photo or screen snip of any physical document and hand it straight to AI.
📋 Simplify for Struggling Readers  
Here is a [GRADE] assignment: [PASTE YOUR ASSIGNMENT]. Rewrite it at a lower reading level (approximately 2 grades below). Keep all the same content and questions, but use shorter sentences, simpler vocabulary, and add visual cues like bold key words.
📋 Extend for Advanced Students  
Here is a [GRADE] assignment: [PASTE YOUR ASSIGNMENT]. Create an extended version for advanced students. Add 2–3 higher-order thinking questions that require analysis, evaluation, or creative application of the concepts.
📋 Adapt for English Learners  
Here is a [GRADE] assignment: [PASTE YOUR ASSIGNMENT]. Adapt it for English Language Learners. Simplify sentence structures, define key vocabulary words in parentheses, and add sentence frames or word banks where helpful.
⚠️ Always review AI-generated assignments! Check for factual accuracy, age-appropriateness, and whether it fits what you're actually teaching before using with students. AI is a great starting point — you make the final call.
Step 6 of 6 — Math & Quick Wins

➗ Math Assignments — What AI Can and Can't Do

AI is genuinely useful for math — but it has real limitations worth knowing before you invest time in it.

✅ Where AI does well:

  • Word problems — AI writes excellent story-based math problems at any grade level and difficulty.
  • Multiple choice math quizzes — works great for concepts, vocabulary, and application questions.
  • Review packets — AI can write sets of practice problems for operations, fractions, place value, geometry vocabulary, and more.
  • Answer keys — ask AI to solve the problems it creates and show its work.
📋 Math Word Problems  
Write 8 math word problems about [TOPIC, e.g., "multiplying fractions" or "calculating area"] for [GRADE] students. Make the scenarios realistic and relatable. Include an answer key with step-by-step solutions.
📋 Math Practice Set  
Create a practice problem set for [GRADE] students on [TOPIC]. Include 10 problems that progress from easier to harder. Format it with space to show work under each problem. Include a separate answer key at the end.

⚠️ Where AI struggles — be honest with yourself:

⚠️ Fractions, equations, and symbols don't always print cleanly. When AI writes math with symbols like ½, ÷, ², or √ — or lays out long division or multi-step equations — the formatting can look messy or broken when you copy it into Word or print it. It often comes out as jumbled text rather than a clean math layout.

What to do instead: Tell the AI upfront how you want the math written. For example: "Write all fractions as words — for example, 'three-fourths' instead of the symbol." AI will follow that instruction every time, and your document will print cleanly without any symbol problems.

🧪 Try it — Build a Math Worksheet

Use the prompt below to create a ready-to-print worksheet. Notice how it tells the AI to write fractions as words — that's the tip from above put to work right away.

📋 Math Worksheet  
Create a math worksheet for [GRADE] students practicing [TOPIC, e.g., "adding fractions" or "long division"]. Include 12 problems that go from easier to harder. Write all fractions as words (for example, "three-fourths" instead of the symbol). Leave space under each problem for students to show their work. Add a simple answer key at the end on a separate section labeled "Teacher Answer Key."

⚡ Quick-Win Prompts for Every Day

These small but powerful prompts can save you 10–20 minutes every single day:

📋 Exit Ticket  
Write 3 exit ticket questions for a [GRADE] lesson about [TOPIC]. One should check basic understanding, one should ask students to explain their thinking, and one should connect to their own lives.
📋 Warm-Up / Bell Ringer  
Create 5 different warm-up activities for a [GRADE] class starting a unit on [TOPIC]. Each should take about 3–5 minutes and activate prior knowledge or curiosity.
📋 Vocabulary List with Definitions  
Create a vocabulary list for a [GRADE] unit on [TOPIC]. Include 10 key words, a student-friendly definition for each, and an example sentence. Format it as a table.
📋 Discussion Questions  
Write 8 discussion questions about [TOPIC/BOOK/ARTICLE] for [GRADE] students. Include a mix of factual, inferential, and open-ended questions. Label each type.
