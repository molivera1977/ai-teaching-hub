Step 2 of 9 — Creating Quizzes

✅ Part 1 — Creating Quizzes

AI can write a full quiz in seconds. Here's how:

  1. Pick a prompt below and left-click Copy.
  2. Open your AI tool in a new tab, right-click inside the chat box, and choose Paste. (You can also press Ctrl+V on Windows or Cmd+V on Mac.)
  3. Replace anything in [brackets] with your own subject, grade, or topic.
  4. Hit Enter — your quiz is ready in seconds.
📋 Multiple Choice Quiz  
Create a 10-question multiple choice quiz about [TOPIC] for [GRADE] students. Each question should have 4 answer choices. Include an answer key at the end.
📋 Short Answer Quiz  
Write a 5-question short-answer quiz about [TOPIC] for [GRADE] students. Questions should require students to explain their thinking, not just recall facts. Include a sample answer for each question.
📋 True/False + Correction  
Create a 10-question true/false quiz about [TOPIC] for [GRADE]. For every false statement, students must rewrite it to make it true. Include an answer key.

Step 3 of 9 — Reading Passages & Question Sets

📖 Reading Passages & Question Sets

AI can write a passage on any topic at any grade level — and pair it with questions that target exactly the skill you're working on. Great for ELA, science, and social studies.

  1. Pick a prompt below and left-click Copy.
  2. Open your AI tool in a new tab, right-click in the chat box, and choose Paste.
  3. Replace anything in [brackets] with your subject, grade, topic, or length.
  4. Hit Enter — copy the result into a Word doc or Google Doc to print or share.

📄 Passages

📋 Informational Passage  
Write an informational reading passage about [TOPIC] for [GRADE] students. The passage should be approximately [LENGTH, e.g. 300] words. Use clear, engaging language appropriate for this grade level. Include a title.
📋 Narrative / Story  
Write a short fictional story for [GRADE] students that teaches the concept of [TOPIC/THEME]. The story should be about [LENGTH] words and include relatable characters and a clear resolution.
📋 Two-Sided Debate Passage  
Write a two-sided informational passage about [TOPIC] for [GRADE] students. Present one paragraph arguing for one side and one paragraph for the other. Use balanced, factual language — do not take a position.

❓ Question Sets — add these after any passage

📋 Overall Comprehension Questions  
Using the passage above, write 6 comprehension questions for [GRADE] students. Include a mix of: 2 literal questions (directly stated in the text), 2 inferential questions (requires reading between the lines), and 2 evaluative questions (opinion or judgment supported by evidence). Label each type.
📋 Main Idea & Supporting Details  
Using the passage above, write 4 questions that focus specifically on main idea and supporting details. Include: 1 question asking students to identify the main idea, 2 questions asking them to identify a key supporting detail, and 1 question asking them to explain how a detail supports the main idea. Write for [GRADE] students.
📋 Text Evidence Practice  
Using the passage above, write 4 questions that require students to cite text evidence in their answers. Each question should include the instruction "Use evidence from the text to support your answer." Make the questions open-ended and appropriate for [GRADE] students.
📋 Author's Purpose & Point of View  
Using the passage above, write 4 questions focused on author's purpose and point of view for [GRADE] students. Include questions about: why the author wrote this, what perspective they are writing from, word choices that reveal the author's attitude, and how a different point of view might change the passage.
📋 Vocabulary in Context  
Using the passage above, identify 5 vocabulary words that are important for [GRADE] students to understand. For each word: quote the sentence it appears in, write a student-friendly definition, and write one question asking students to use context clues to figure out its meaning.
✅ Pro tip — reading levels: You can ask AI to match a specific reading level. Just say "beginner," "on grade level," or "advanced" — or if your school uses Lexile scores, be precise: "Write this at approximately a 600L Lexile level." (600L ≈ 4th grade, 970L ≈ 7th grade.)
Step 4 of 9 — Creating Rubrics

📊 Part 3 — Creating Rubrics

Rubrics used to take forever to build from scratch. AI can produce a complete, detailed rubric in about 30 seconds. You can then tweak it to match your exact expectations.

  1. Pick a prompt below and left-click Copy.
  2. Open your AI tool in a new tab, right-click in the chat box, and choose Paste.
  3. Replace anything in [brackets] with your assignment type, grade, or categories.
  4. Hit Enter — then copy the result into a Word doc or Google Doc to print or share.
📋 Essay Rubric  
Create a 4-point rubric for grading a [GRADE] opinion essay. Include the following categories: Claim/Thesis, Evidence & Support, Organization, and Language/Conventions. Format it as a table with clear descriptions for each score level (4=Exceeds, 3=Meets, 2=Approaching, 1=Beginning).
📋 Project Rubric  
Create a rubric for a [GRADE] student project about [TOPIC]. The project involves [DESCRIBE THE PROJECT]. Include 4 categories that are most important for this assignment. Use a 1–4 scale with clear descriptions at each level.
📋 Presentation Rubric  
Write a rubric for evaluating a student oral presentation for [GRADE]. Include categories for: Content Knowledge, Organization, Speaking Clearly, and Visual Aids. Use a 4-point scale and student-friendly language so students can self-assess.
Step 5 of 9 — Lesson Plans

🗓️ Part 5 — Lesson Plans

Writing a lesson plan from scratch takes time you don't always have. AI can build a solid first draft in seconds — then you adjust it to fit your class. Here's how:

  1. Pick a prompt below and left-click Copy.
  2. Open your AI tool in a new tab, right-click in the chat box, and choose Paste.
  3. Replace anything in [brackets] with your subject, grade, topic, or time.
  4. Hit Enter — read through the plan and tweak anything that doesn't fit your class.
📋 Full Lesson Plan  
Write a complete lesson plan for a [GRADE] [SUBJECT] class on [TOPIC]. The lesson should take [TIME, e.g., "45 minutes"]. Include: an objective, a warm-up activity, direct instruction, a guided practice activity, an independent practice task, and a closing/exit ticket. Note any materials needed.
📋 Mini-Lesson (15–20 Minutes)  
Write a 15-minute mini-lesson for [GRADE] students on [TOPIC]. Include a hook to grab attention, a clear explanation of the key concept, one quick guided example, and a 2-minute check for understanding at the end.
📋 Sub Plan  
Write a substitute teacher plan for a [GRADE] [SUBJECT] class. The sub should be able to run the entire class without needing any special knowledge of the subject. Include: a greeting script to read to the class, a review activity on [TOPIC], independent work time with clear instructions, and a short class discussion or reflection to close. Keep instructions simple and step-by-step.
📋 Week-at-a-Glance Unit Outline  
Create a 5-day unit outline for [GRADE] [SUBJECT] on [TOPIC]. For each day include: the daily objective, the main activity, and how the lesson will end. Keep it brief — this is an overview, not a full plan for each day.
⚠️ Always review before using. AI doesn't know your students, your pacing guide, or what you covered last week. Treat the output as a strong first draft — not a finished plan. Adjust the timing, activities, and language to fit your actual class.
Step 6 of 9 — Math Assignments

➗ Math Assignments — What AI Can and Can't Do

AI is genuinely useful for math — but it has real limitations worth knowing before you invest time in it.

✅ Where AI does well:

  • Word problems — AI writes excellent story-based math problems at any grade level and difficulty.
  • Multiple choice math quizzes — works great for concepts, vocabulary, and application questions.
  • Review packets — AI can write sets of practice problems for operations, fractions, place value, geometry vocabulary, and more.
  • Answer keys — ask AI to solve the problems it creates and show its work.
📋 Math Word Problems  
Write 8 math word problems about [TOPIC, e.g., "multiplying fractions" or "calculating area"] for [GRADE] students. Make the scenarios realistic and relatable. Include an answer key with step-by-step solutions.
📋 Math Practice Set  
Create a practice problem set for [GRADE] students on [TOPIC]. Include 10 problems that progress from easier to harder. Format it with space to show work under each problem. Include a separate answer key at the end.
💡 Before you print — two quick reminders:
  • Check the answer key yourself. AI reasons through math and can occasionally get a multi-step problem wrong — a quick review before it reaches students is always worth it.
  • Formatting looks off? Ask for an HTML file instead. Fractions and symbols can get jumbled when pasted into Word. Add this line to any math prompt: "Give me this as an HTML file I can open in my browser and print." It handles the layout cleanly every time.

🧪 Try it — Build a Math Worksheet

Use the prompt below to create a ready-to-print worksheet. The second version asks for an HTML file — try both and see which result looks better.

📋 Math Worksheet (plain text version)  
Create a math worksheet for [GRADE] students practicing [TOPIC, e.g., "adding fractions" or "long division"]. Include 12 problems that go from easier to harder. Write all fractions as words (for example, "three-fourths" instead of the symbol). Leave space under each problem for students to show their work. Add a simple answer key at the end on a separate section labeled "Teacher Answer Key."
⭐ Math Worksheet (HTML file — recommended for math)  
Create a math worksheet for [GRADE] students practicing [TOPIC, e.g., "adding fractions" or "long division"]. Include 12 problems that go from easier to harder. Leave space under each problem for students to show their work. Add a teacher answer key at the end. Give me this as a complete HTML file I can open in my browser and print — make it visually clean with proper spacing, a title at the top, and a name/date line.

Step 8 of 9 — Brainstorming

💡 Brainstorming with AI

One of the most underused things teachers can do with AI — use it as a thinking partner. Give it a topic or a problem and ask it to generate ideas. You won't use everything it gives you, but you'll almost always find something worth keeping.

  1. Pick a prompt below and left-click Copy.
  2. Open your AI tool in a new tab, right-click in the chat box, and choose Paste.
  3. Replace anything in [brackets] with your subject, grade, topic, or goal.
  4. Hit Enter — scan the ideas and grab anything that sparks something. Ask AI to expand on any idea you like.
📋 Lesson Hook Ideas  
Give me 8 creative hook ideas to kick off a [GRADE] lesson on [TOPIC]. Each should take under 5 minutes and get students curious or thinking before I've taught anything. Make each one different — include a mix of questions, challenges, videos, demos, and discussion starters.
📋 Project or Activity Ideas  
Brainstorm 10 project or activity ideas for a [GRADE] unit on [TOPIC]. Include a mix of individual and group options, and a mix of creative, research-based, and hands-on formats. Keep each description to 2–3 sentences.
📋 Icebreakers & Community Builders  
Give me 6 icebreaker or community-building activities for a [GRADE] class. They should be quick (5–10 minutes), require no materials, and work for students who don't know each other well yet. Include a mix of low-pressure and slightly more interactive options.
📋 Ways to Introduce a Concept  
I need to introduce [CONCEPT] to [GRADE] students who have never seen it before. Give me 6 different ways I could explain or introduce it — use analogies, real-world examples, visuals, stories, and hands-on approaches. I'll pick the one that fits my class best.
📋 Unit Planning Ideas  
I'm planning a unit on [TOPIC] for [GRADE] students. Brainstorm ideas for: a compelling essential question, 3–5 key understandings students should walk away with, possible performance tasks or final projects, and real-world connections I could make throughout the unit.
Step 9 of 9 — Parent Communication

✉️ Parent Communication

Writing the same type of message over and over is one of the biggest hidden time drains in teaching. AI can draft newsletters, progress updates, and difficult emails in seconds — you just personalize and send.

  1. Pick a prompt below and left-click Copy.
  2. Open your AI tool in a new tab, right-click in the chat box, and choose Paste.
  3. Replace anything in [brackets] with your details — student name, topic, grade, etc.
  4. Hit Enter. Read the result, adjust the tone or details to match your voice, then copy it into your email or newsletter.
📋 Class Newsletter  
Write a friendly, professional class newsletter for [GRADE] parents. This week we covered [TOPICS]. Upcoming: [EVENTS OR DATES]. Remind parents about [REMINDER, e.g., "picture day Friday" or "field trip permission slips"]. Keep it warm, brief, and easy to read — about 150 words.
📋 Positive Progress Note  
Write a short, warm email to the parent of [STUDENT NAME], a [GRADE] student. I want to share that [he/she/they] has been doing well — specifically [SPECIFIC STRENGTH OR IMPROVEMENT, e.g., "showing great effort in writing" or "helping classmates during group work"]. Keep it genuine and encouraging, about 3–4 sentences.
📋 Academic Concern Email  
Help me write a professional, caring email to the parent of [STUDENT NAME], a [GRADE] student. I want to share a concern about [ISSUE, e.g., "missing assignments" or "struggling with reading comprehension"]. I'd like to suggest [NEXT STEP, e.g., "a brief phone call" or "extra reading practice at home"]. Keep the tone supportive, not alarming — about 4–5 sentences.
📋 Behavior Concern Email  
Write a professional, calm email to the parent of [STUDENT NAME] about a behavior concern. The issue is [DESCRIBE BEHAVIOR, e.g., "difficulty staying focused during instruction" or "a conflict with a classmate"]. I want to keep the parent informed and work together. Suggest we [NEXT STEP, e.g., "check in by phone this week"]. Tone: factual, not accusatory, about 4–5 sentences.
📋 Event or Field Trip Announcement  
Write a parent announcement for [EVENT, e.g., "our class field trip to the science museum"]. Date: [DATE]. Students need to bring: [ITEMS]. Permission slips are due by [DATE]. Cost: [AMOUNT or "no cost"]. Keep it friendly, clear, and under 120 words.
💡 Make it sound like you. AI drafts are a starting point — always read through and swap in your own phrasing before sending. Parents will notice (and appreciate) when it sounds genuine.
