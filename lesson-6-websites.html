🤯 AI Can Build You a Website — Really

When most teachers hear "build a website," they picture months of learning, expensive software, and headaches. With AI, it's completely different.

📌 Here's all you need to know: You describe what you want in plain English. AI writes all the behind-the-scenes code. You get a working website — without ever seeing or touching a single line of code.

Think of it like being an architect. An architect doesn't swing a hammer or lay bricks — they describe what the building should look like, and a builder brings it to life. You are the architect. AI is the builder.

The same AI tools you've been using throughout this course — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — can build complete, working websites. All you have to do is describe what you want.

✅ You already know how to do this. Every skill from this course — writing clear prompts, being specific, asking for revisions — applies here. You're already prepared.

Step 3 of 7 — How to Ask

💬 How to Ask AI to Build a Website

The process is simpler than you think. Three things to include in your request:

  1. What type of site — a quiz, a flashcard game, a class homepage, etc.
  2. Who it's for — your grade level and subject
  3. What it should include — the content, questions, links, colors, or style you want
✅ One important phrase to always include: End your request with "Put everything in one HTML file." An HTML file is just a web page file — it opens in any browser (Chrome, Safari, Edge) with a double-click, and anyone can view it. This keeps things simple and easy to share.

A simple formula that works every time:

"Build me a [type of site] for [grade] students about [topic]. Include [list what you want]. Put everything in one HTML file."

That's it. You don't need to say anything technical. AI figures out the rest.

After AI builds it:

  1. AI gives you the finished site — either a file to download or a preview to view.
  2. Open it in your browser and see how it looks.
  3. If anything needs changing, just say so in plain English. Keep refining until it's perfect.

Step 5 of 7 — Sharing With Students

🔗 Sharing Your Site With Students

Once AI builds your site and gives you the HTML file, here are the three ways to get it in front of students. Pick the one that fits your situation.

📁

Option 1 — Google Drive (great for Google schools)

Google Drive is Google's online file storage — if your school uses Google, you already have a Google Drive account. Think of it as a filing cabinet in the cloud that you can access from any computer.

  1. Go to drive.google.com — type that address into your browser's address bar and press Enter. Sign in with your school Google account if it asks.
  2. Find the blue "New" button on the left side of the page and click it. A small menu drops down. Click "File upload." A window opens showing your computer's files — find your HTML file (check your Downloads folder), and double-click it to upload it.
  3. Once it uploads, find the file in the list. Right-click it (right mouse button, or two-finger tap on a Mac) and choose "Share." A sharing window opens.
  4. Look for where it says "Restricted" and click the dropdown arrow next to it. Choose "Anyone with the link." Then check that the box to the right says "Viewer" — this means students can open and see it but can't accidentally change anything.
  5. Click "Copy link." The link is now on your clipboard, ready to paste. Go to Google Classroom (your school's online classroom tool where you post assignments and materials for students), click where you want to add it, and press Ctrl+V (Windows) or Cmd+V (Mac) to paste. Students click the link and the site opens in their browser. Done.
💡 Tip: Students don't need a Google account to open it — anyone with the link can view it.
💼

Option 2 — Microsoft Teams (great for Microsoft schools)

Microsoft Teams is a communication and classroom tool that Microsoft schools use. It's organized into classes — each class has its own space where you can post messages, files, and assignments for students.

  1. Open Microsoft Teams and click on your class — the list of your classes appears on the left side. Click the one where you want to share.
  2. Click "Files" near the top of the screen. This is the tab where you store and share documents with students. You'll see a row of tabs — Posts, Files, Assignments, etc. Click Files.
  3. Click "Upload" and find your HTML file — a window opens showing your computer's files. Find your HTML file (check your Downloads folder), double-click it, and Teams stores it in the class folder.
  4. Right-click the file and choose "Copy link." Then click on Posts (the general chat area all students see), click where you'd type a message, and press Ctrl+V (Windows) or Cmd+V (Mac) to paste the link. Students click it and the site opens in their browser.
  5. Students click the link — the file opens in their browser. Done.
💡 Tip: You can also attach the HTML file directly to a Teams Assignment — when you create an assignment, look for an "Add resources" or "Attach" option and attach the file there so students get it as part of their task.
📺

Option 3 — Project it on the Board (no sharing needed, no accounts needed)

  1. Find your HTML file on your computer — it's wherever you saved it (Downloads, Desktop, etc.).
  2. Double-click it — it opens in your browser automatically.
  3. Connect your computer to the projector — go full-screen and the class sees it on the board.
💡 Best for: Whole-class review, walking students through something together, or demoing before they work independently.
🌐

Option 4 — Put it Online (permanent link, free — takes ~10 minutes to set up the first time)

This gives your site a real web address — like a website anyone can visit anytime. It's the most powerful option but takes a few extra minutes to set up the first time.

📌 What is GitHub? GitHub is a free service (like Google Drive, but for websites and code) where you can store files and make them live on the internet. You don't need to understand how it works — you just need a free account. Think of it as the "publish" button for your site.
  1. Go to github.com and create a free account — use your personal email (not your school email, which may block outside services).
  2. Ask AI to guide you — type: "Walk me through putting an HTML file on GitHub Pages step by step." It will give you exact instructions for what you see on screen.
  3. Follow the steps — upload your file, click a few buttons, and GitHub gives you a permanent link (something like "yourname.github.io/my-quiz").
  4. Share that link anywhere — paste it in Google Classroom, email it to students or parents, or turn it into a QR code (a square scannable barcode — students point their phone camera at it and the site opens instantly). A free tool at qr-code-generator.com turns any link into a QR code in seconds. Your link never expires.
📌 Don't worry if it sounds technical — the AI walks you through every click. Most teachers finish in under 10 minutes the first time, and it's instant every time after that.

🚀 Want AI to Do Absolutely Everything?

Everything in this lesson so far still requires a small amount of manual work — copying code, saving a file, uploading to Google Drive. But there's a next level where you don't do any of that.

There's a version of Claude called Claude Code that doesn't just give you instructions — it actually takes action on your computer. It creates the files, saves them, puts the site online, and hands you a live link. All you do is describe what you want.

📌 Think of it this way: Regular AI is like a contractor who reads blueprints and tells you exactly how to build the house. Claude Code is like a contractor who actually shows up and builds the house while you watch.

📥 Step 1 — Download the Claude Code Desktop App

There are two ways to run Claude Code. The desktop app is the friendly one — it looks just like a regular chat window, no scary screens involved.

  1. Open claude.ai/download — go there in any browser on your Mac or Windows computer.
  2. Click "Download for Mac" (or Windows) — open the setup file when it finishes downloading. It walks you through a simple Next → Next → Install process, just like any other app.
  3. Drag Claude into your Applications folder — then double-click to open it.
  4. Sign in with your existing claude.ai account — no new account needed. You're ready.
💡 Already use Claude? You may already have the desktop app. Look in your Applications folder or Dock for the Claude icon. If it's there, you're halfway done.

🔐 Step 2 — Grant the Permissions It Needs

Because Claude Code actually does things on your computer (creating files, opening folders), your Mac or Windows machine will ask you to approve a few permissions. This is normal and safe — it's the same kind of pop-up you see when any new app wants access.

  • Allow access to your folders — so it can create and save your website files.
  • Allow it to take actions on your screen — a permission pop-up will appear (like the kind you see when any new app needs access to something). Click Allow. This is what lets it create and save your files for you instead of just giving you instructions to do it yourself.
  • Allow it to open your browser — so it can show you the finished site when it's done.
⚠️ Only approve Claude Code — these permissions are for the official Claude app from Anthropic. Never grant "control your computer" access to a random website or unknown app.

🛠️ Step 3 — Describe Your Site and Watch It Build

Once it's set up, using Claude Code is exactly like chatting with regular Claude — except it builds instead of just talks.

  1. Open Claude Code and type your request — it looks like a chat window. Type in plain English: "Build me a 10-question quiz site about the water cycle for 4th graders."
  2. Watch it work — you'll see it creating files and writing code automatically. You don't touch anything. It's like watching a fast-forward video of someone building your site.
  3. It publishes the site for you — Claude Code connects to GitHub (a free hosting service) and puts your site online with a real web address you can share.
  4. You get a live link — copy it, paste it into Google Classroom, and your students can open it immediately. Done.
✅ You've already seen the result. This entire teaching hub was built exactly this way — described in plain English, built by Claude Code, published automatically. Not a single line of code was written by hand.

🆚 How Is This Different From Regular AI?

Every AI tool can build a website for you. The difference is what you still have to do yourself after it's done:

Tool Builds your site You still save & share it yourself
ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, Claude ✅ Yes ✅ Yes — you handle that part
Claude Code (with permission) ✅ Yes ✅ No — it handles that too

That's the whole idea. You describe what you want, and Claude Code takes care of everything else — including getting it in front of your students.
