Step 1 of 5 — Your Browser & Tabs
👋 Welcome! This short lesson covers the basic computer skills you'll use throughout this course. Even if some of it is already familiar to you, it's worth a quick read — everyone picks up a useful trick or two. Go at your own pace.

🌐 What Is a Browser?

The program you are using right now to read this page is called a web browser — or just "browser." It's the window that lets you visit websites on the internet.

The most common browsers are:

  • Google Chrome — the most popular, works on Windows and Mac
  • Microsoft Edge — comes pre-installed on most Windows computers
  • Safari — comes pre-installed on all Mac computers and iPhones
  • Firefox — another popular option
📌 It doesn't matter which browser you use. All the AI tools in this course work in any of them. If you're not sure which one you have, look at the icon you clicked to get here — that's your browser.
🤔 Not sure if you have Windows or Mac? Here's the easiest way to tell:
  • Windows: Your screen probably has a taskbar along the bottom with a Windows logo (⊞) in the corner, and your keyboard likely has a key labeled Ctrl.
  • Mac: You'll see a row of colored icons along the bottom (called the Dock), an Apple logo (🍎) in the top-left corner, and your keyboard has a key labeled ⌘ Command (or Cmd).
You'll need to know this because some keyboard shortcuts use different keys on Windows vs. Mac — we'll always tell you both.

📋 The Address Bar

At the very top of your browser window is a long white box. That's the address bar — it shows the web address (called a URL) of the page you're on. You type web addresses there to go somewhere new.

For example: to open Claude, you'd click in that box, type claude.ai, and press Enter.

➕ Opening a New Tab

A tab is like a separate page open inside your browser — you can have many at once without opening a whole new window. You'll open new tabs constantly in this course.

1
Look at the very top of your browser — you'll see your open tabs. They look like small rectangles with the page title inside. The one you're on right now is highlighted.
2
To open a new tab, click the ＋ button next to your last tab. A new blank tab opens. Or use a keyboard shortcut — that means pressing two keys at the same time to make something happen quickly, instead of clicking a button with your mouse: Ctrl + T on Windows, or ⌘ Cmd + T on Mac.
3
To switch between tabs, just click on any tab at the top. You can come back to this lesson anytime by clicking its tab.
✅ Try it now: Open a new tab (Ctrl+T or Cmd+T), then click back on this tab. You just did what every lesson will ask you to do!
Step 2 of 5 — Clicking & Scrolling

🖱️ Left Click vs. Right Click

Your mouse (or trackpad) has two sides. They do different things:

Left Click 👆

The button you use for almost everything — opening links, selecting text, pressing buttons. When someone says "click," they mean left click.

Right Click 🖱️

Opens a small menu with extra options — like Copy, Paste, Save image as, and more. You'll use this a lot in this course.

📌 On a Mac trackpad: Right-click = tap with two fingers at the same time, or hold Ctrl and tap. On a Mac mouse with only one button: hold the Control key and click.

📜 Scrolling

Scrolling means moving up and down a page to see more content. There's more on this page than fits on your screen at once — scrolling lets you see it all.

  • Mouse wheel: Roll the little wheel between your mouse buttons up or down.
  • Trackpad: Slide two fingers up or down.
  • Keyboard: Press the down arrow key, or the Page Down key to jump further.
  • Scrollbar: Look for a thin bar on the very right edge of your browser window — click and drag it down.

🖥️ Clicking Inside a Text Box

Throughout this course you'll need to click inside boxes where you type — like the typing box in Claude or ChatGPT where you send your messages. This is called placing your cursor.

When you click inside a text box, you'll see a thin blinking line appear. That line is your cursor — it shows where your typing will appear. If you don't see the blinking line, click once more inside the box.

Step 3 of 5 — Copy & Paste

📋 What Is Copy & Paste?

Copying and pasting is one of the most useful skills on a computer. It lets you take text (or an image) from one place and put an exact copy of it somewhere else — without retyping anything.

Think of it like this: Copy is like using a photocopier. Paste is like putting that copy down somewhere new. The original stays exactly where it was.

Step 1 — Highlight the Text

Before you can copy anything, you have to tell your computer which text you want. You do this by highlighting it — it turns blue when selected.

Option A — Click and drag (pick exactly what you want)

Use this when you only want part of the document — a paragraph, a section, or a few sentences:

  1. Click your mouse right before the first word you want — put your cursor at the very start of that text.
  2. Hold the mouse button down and slowly drag to the right and down until you reach the last word you want.
  3. Let go of the mouse button. Everything you dragged over will turn blue — that means it's selected.
  4. If you went too far or not far enough, just click somewhere and try again.
Option B — Keyboard shortcut (selects everything)

Click anywhere inside the text, then press:

Ctrl+A on Windows · ⌘ Cmd+A on Mac

This selects everything in the document all at once — the whole thing, top to bottom. Useful when you want it all, but keep in mind it may include headers, footers, or parts you didn't mean to copy.

How you'll know it worked: The text turns blue (highlighted). If nothing turns blue, click once inside the text area and try again.

Step 2 — Copy It

While the text is highlighted (blue), copy it:

Option A — Right-click

While the text is highlighted, press the right button on your mouse. A small menu appears on screen. Click the word "Copy" in that menu.

Option B — Keyboard shortcut
Ctrl+C on Windows · ⌘ Cmd+C on Mac

Nothing visible will happen — that is completely normal. Your text is now invisibly held by your computer. It's ready to place somewhere new.

Step 3 — Paste It

Click inside the place where you want the text to appear (like a chat box). Then paste:

Option A — Right-click

Left-click inside the destination box so your cursor is there. Then right-click and choose "Paste" from the menu.

Option B — Keyboard shortcut
Ctrl+V on Windows · ⌘ Cmd+V on Mac

How you'll know it worked: The text appears wherever your cursor was. The same text is now in two places — the original stayed where it was, and a copy appeared in the new spot.

Step 4 of 5 — Finding Your Downloads

📥 What Happens When You Download Something?

When you click a download button or link on a website, your computer saves that file for you automatically. Many teachers don't realize the file is just sitting quietly in a folder called Downloads — waiting to be found.

📌 Nothing got lost. If you've ever downloaded something and then couldn't find it — it's almost certainly in your Downloads folder. Here's how to find it.

🪟 Finding Downloads on Windows

1
Look at the bottom of your screen — find the folder icon in your taskbar. It looks like a yellow folder. Click it. This opens File Explorer — your computer's file manager. A file manager is a program that shows all the files and folders on your computer, organized so you can find and open them.
2
Look at the left side of the window — click "Downloads." You'll see a list of folders on the left side of the window. "Downloads" is usually near the top, under a section called "Quick Access" — that's just Windows' name for your most-used folders, shown at the top for easy finding.
3
Your downloaded files are listed here, newest at the top. Double-click any file to open it. Word documents open in Microsoft Word, PDFs open automatically in your browser, and HTML files also open in your browser. Your computer figures out which program to use on its own.
✅ Shortcut: After downloading something, look at the bottom of your browser — a small bar usually appears showing the file you just downloaded. You can click it directly from there without having to find the folder.

🍎 Finding Downloads on Mac

1
Look at the bottom of your screen — find the Finder icon in your Dock. It looks like a blue-and-white smiley face. Click it. Finder is Mac's file manager — the program that lets you see and open all the files on your computer.
2
Look at the left sidebar — click "Downloads." A list of your downloaded files appears. Newest files are at the top.
3
Double-click any file to open it. Your Mac picks the right program automatically. Word documents usually open in Microsoft Word, or in Pages if you don't have Word (Pages is Apple's free built-in writing program — it works the same way). PDFs open in Preview, which is Mac's built-in document viewer, or in your browser. HTML files open in Safari or Chrome.
✅ Shortcut: After downloading something in Safari or Chrome on a Mac, look for a small arrow icon (⬇) near the top-right of your browser. Click it to see your recent downloads and open them directly.

📂 What Do File Names Mean?

Files have a name and then a short ending (called an extension) that tells your computer what kind of file it is. Here are the ones you'll see in this course:

.docx
Microsoft Word document
Opens in Word. Great for assignments, lesson plans, and anything you want to edit later.
.pdf
PDF document
Opens right in your browser. Looks exactly the same on every computer — can't be accidentally changed. Perfect for printing and sharing.
.html
Web page file
Double-click it and it opens in your browser like a website. AI can build these for you!
.png .jpg
Image files
Photos and illustrations. Open by double-clicking — your computer picks the right app automatically.
Step 5 of 5 — Key Vocabulary

📖 Words You'll See in This Course

These are the tech words that come up again and again. You don't need to memorize them — just know you can come back here anytime one appears and you're not sure what it means.

Browser

The program you use to visit websites — Chrome, Safari, Edge, or Firefox. You're inside one right now.

Tab

A separate page open inside your browser. You can have many tabs open at once — click them at the top of the browser to switch between them.

URL / Web address / Link

The address of a website, like claude.ai or chatgpt.com. You type it in the address bar at the top of your browser, or click it when it's underlined.

Prompt

What you type to an AI. It's just your question or instruction, written in plain English. "Write a quiz about volcanoes for 5th graders" is a prompt.

Chat box

The box at the bottom of Claude or ChatGPT where you type your message. Click inside it, type your prompt, and press Enter to send.

Clipboard

An invisible holding area on your computer. When you copy something (Ctrl+C), it goes to the clipboard and waits there until you paste it somewhere.

Highlight / Select

When text turns blue after you click and drag over it (or press Ctrl+A), that means it's highlighted — your computer knows that's the text you want to work with.

Download

Saving a file from the internet onto your computer. When you click a "Download" button, the file saves automatically to your Downloads folder.

Upload

Sending a file from your computer to a website. When you attach a file to a chat, you're uploading it.

Attach / Paperclip

The 📎 paperclip icon in Claude or ChatGPT lets you attach a file from your computer — a Word doc, PDF, or image — so AI can read it.

Account / Log in / Sign in

Most AI tools ask you to create a free account (your email + a password). "Log in" and "sign in" mean the same thing — entering your email and password to get into your account.

File / Folder

A file is a single document (like a quiz or a photo). A folder is a container that holds files — like a digital filing cabinet drawer.

Scroll

Moving up or down a page to see more content. Roll your mouse wheel, slide two fingers on a trackpad, or drag the thin bar on the right edge of the window.

Screenshot / Snip

A photo of whatever is on your screen at that moment. You can take one with a keyboard shortcut and then share it with AI. Lesson 3 covers this in detail.

HTML file

A web page file that lives on your computer. Double-click it and it opens in your browser like a website — great for quizzes and worksheets that look clean when printed.

Extension (file)

The short ending of a file name — like .pdf, .docx, or .html — that tells your computer what kind of file it is and which program to use to open it.
