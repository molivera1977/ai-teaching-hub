Step 1 of 6 — What Is a Prompt?
🚀 Before you begin: Open one of these in a new tab — all are free to start. You'll use it to try the examples as you go through this lesson.
Claude or ChatGPT or Gemini or Copilot

🤔 What Is a Prompt?

When you type something to an AI — a question, a request, an instruction — that's called a prompt. It's just what you type into the chat box.

Think of prompting like giving instructions to a very capable substitute teacher. If you're vague, they'll make their best guess (and it might not be what you wanted). If you're specific and clear, they'll do exactly what you need.

📌 Key idea: The quality of what AI gives you is almost entirely determined by the quality of your prompt. Better prompt = better result.
Step 2 of 6 — The Parts of a Good Prompt

⚡ The Parts of a Good Prompt

A strong prompt usually has some or all of these parts. You don't need all of them every time, but the more context you give, the better the result.

1
Your Role (who you are)
Tell the AI a bit about yourself — it helps match the right tone and level.
"I am a 4th grade teacher…"
2
The Task (what you want)
Be direct. Use action words like write, create, list, summarize, explain, rewrite.
"…create a short reading passage…"
3
The Details (specifics that matter)
Grade level, subject, length, format, tone — anything that shapes the output.
"…about the water cycle, written at a 4th grade reading level, about 150 words long."
4
The Audience (who is this for?)
Mentioning your students helps AI adjust vocabulary and complexity.
"…My students are 9–10 years old and this is their first introduction to the topic."
✅ All 4 parts together:

"I am a 4th grade teacher. Create a short reading passage about the water cycle, written at a 4th grade reading level, about 150 words long. My students are 9–10 years old and this is their first introduction to the topic."
Step 3 of 6 — Weak vs. Strong Prompts

🔍 Weak Prompts vs. Strong Prompts

Here are some real examples of the same idea asked two different ways. Notice how much better the specific version works:

❌ Weak Prompt✅ Strong Prompt
"Write something about fractions." "Write a 5-question multiple choice quiz about adding fractions with unlike denominators for 5th grade students."
"Give me a lesson idea." "Give me 3 engaging lesson hook ideas for teaching 3rd graders about animal adaptations. Each idea should take under 5 minutes."
"Make it simpler." "Rewrite this paragraph at a 2nd grade reading level, keeping the same main idea but using shorter sentences and simpler vocabulary."
"Write a rubric." "Create a 4-point rubric for a 4th grade opinion essay. Include categories for: claim, evidence, organization, and conventions."
Step 4 of 6 — Prompting Strategies

🛠️ 5 Prompting Strategies for Teachers

1
Give it a role to play

Starting with "Act as a [type of expert]" focuses AI's perspective — like asking a specialist instead of a generalist.

Try this
"Act as an experienced elementary school literacy coach. Help me write a parent letter explaining our new reading program in simple, friendly language."
2
Ask for options, not just one answer

AI is a great brainstorming partner. Ask for multiple versions so you can pick the one that fits best.

Try this
"Give me 5 different ways to introduce the concept of multiplication to 3rd graders. Make each idea different from the others."
3
Tell it what format you want

Bullet points? A table? Numbered list? Just say so — AI will match whatever structure you ask for.

Try this
"List 10 discussion questions about Charlotte's Web. Format them as a numbered list, organized from easiest to most challenging."
4
Ask it to revise

Not happy with the first result? Don't start over — just tell it what to change and it will try again.

Try this
"That's good, but can you make the tone more exciting and add one more question at the end?"
5
Paste in your own text

Hand AI something you've already written — a passage, a rubric, student work — and ask it to work with it directly.

Try this
"Here is a paragraph a student wrote: [paste text here]. Give me 3 specific, kind pieces of feedback to help them improve their writing."

💡 Note about [brackets]: Throughout this course, anything in [square brackets] is a placeholder — replace it with your own words. So [paste text here] means: delete those words and type (or paste) in your actual student paragraph.

📋 Not sure how to copy and paste? No worries — our Start Here lesson walks you through it step by step with a practice exercise. For now, you can also just type the text directly into the typing box — AI doesn't care how it got there.
Step 5 of 6 — Common Mistakes to Avoid

🚫 Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Being too vague: "Help me with my class" tells AI almost nothing. Always say what subject, grade, and what you need.
  • Accepting the first result blindly: Always read the output carefully. AI can get facts wrong, especially specific dates, names, or statistics.
  • Giving up after one try: If the result isn't right, keep the conversation going. Say "try again" or "change this part."
  • Sharing student names or personal data: Never type real student names, birthdates, or personal information into AI tools.
⚠️ Privacy tip: When using AI for student work, use fictional names or just say "a student" instead of using real names. This protects student privacy.
