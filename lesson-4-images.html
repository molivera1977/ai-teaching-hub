🖼️ AI Can Draw — And It's Amazing

AI image generators let you describe any picture in words and the AI creates it for you in seconds. No clipart hunting, no stock photo subscriptions, no design software.

Imagine you need an illustration of a frog adapting to a desert environment, a diagram showing the phases of the moon, or a cartoon scientist for a worksheet header. Just describe it — and it appears.

📌 How it works: You type a description (called a "prompt") and the AI generates a brand-new image that has never existed before. Every image is original and unique to your request.

Step 2 of 6 — The Best Tools

🛠️ The Best AI Image Tools for Teachers

There are several tools available. Here are the most teacher-friendly options:

ChatGPT (chatgpt.com) Free tier available

ChatGPT can generate images directly in the chat. Just ask it to "draw" or "create an image of" something and it will create one for you.

Best for: Detailed illustrations, classroom posters, story scenes, labeled diagrams.

Google Gemini (gemini.google.com) Free tier available

Gemini can generate images right inside the chat. If you use Google Workspace at school, Gemini is already part of your ecosystem and easy to access.

Best for: Educational maps, illustrated scenes, colorful classroom visuals.

Canva AI (canva.com) Free for teachers

Canva is a free, popular design website that many teachers already use to make posters, newsletters, and worksheets — you may have heard of it. It also has built-in AI image generation. The big advantage: create an image AND drop it straight into your design — all in one place, without switching between apps. Canva's full paid version is free for teachers.

Best for: Worksheets, posters, newsletters, and slide decks — when you need the image AND the design in one place.

Perplexity (perplexity.ai) Free tier available

Perplexity lets you generate images using several different AI tools — all from one place. Great for quickly comparing styles or finding the look you want without switching between apps.

Best for: Quickly comparing image styles across different AI models without switching between apps.

Microsoft Copilot (copilot.microsoft.com) Free tier available

Copilot can generate images directly in the chat using Microsoft Designer — Microsoft's built-in image creation tool. If your school uses Microsoft programs, you may already have access.

Best for: Microsoft schools — generate images right inside the tool you already use, no extra accounts needed.

🤔 What about Claude? Claude is great for writing, lesson planning, and text tasks — but it does not generate images. If you ask Claude to "draw" something, it will describe the image in words rather than create a picture. For actual AI image creation, use one of the tools above.
✅ Recommendation for beginners: Start with whichever tool you already use. Google school? Try Gemini. Microsoft school? Try Copilot. Using ChatGPT? It's great too. Once you're comfortable, explore Canva for an all-in-one design + image experience.

Step 3 of 6 — Writing Image Prompts

💬 How to Write a Great Image Prompt

Image prompts work differently from text prompts. Instead of giving instructions, you're describing a picture — what's in it, the style, the mood, and the colors.

The formula for a strong image prompt:

👤 Subject + 🏞️ Setting + 🎨 Style + ✨ Details
📝 Example: "A friendly cartoon elephant reading a book in a library, watercolor illustration style, soft pastel colors, child-friendly"
  1. 🚀 1. Open your AI image tool (like ChatGPT, Adobe Firefly, or Canva AI) and click inside the prompt box.
  2. 👤 2. Start with your subject — who or what is in the image.
  3. 🏞️ 3. Add setting or context — where is it? What is happening around it?
  4. 🎨 4. Add a style word — cartoon, watercolor, realistic, flat, diagram (see style words below).
  5. ✨ 5. Add any extra details — colors, mood, age-appropriateness, or what to leave out.
  6. ↩️ 6. Hit Enter and review the result. If it's not right, describe what to change and send again.

Style words to know:

  • Cartoon / illustrated — fun, colorful, great for younger students
  • Watercolor — soft, artistic, works well for stories and reading corners
  • Realistic — looks like a photograph, good for science topics
  • Flat / minimal — simple, clean shapes with no shadows or textures, great for diagrams and icons
  • Diagram / labeled chart — ask for this when you want educational visuals with labels

Tips for better results:

  • Be descriptive, not vague. Instead of "a dog," try "a golden retriever puppy sitting in a field of sunflowers, happy expression, cartoon illustration style."
  • Say what you DON'T want. Add "no text in the image," "no scary elements," or "simple background" to guide the AI away from unwanted results.
  • Ask for revisions. If it's not right, describe what to change: "Make it more colorful," "Change the background to white," or "Make the character look younger."
  • Generate multiple versions. Ask the AI to "generate 4 different versions" so you have options to choose from.
⚠️ Important — Copyright & Accuracy: AI images are generally safe to use for educational purposes, but always check your school district's policy. Also — AI sometimes adds incorrect details (like wrong number of fingers, or inaccurate historical elements). Always review images before using them with students.

  2. Open one of these AI tools in a new tab:

Canva — Not Quite AI, But Worth Knowing

📌 Why is Canva on its own page?

Canva is not a chat-based AI tool like ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or Copilot. You don't type a question and get an answer. Instead, it's a design app where you build things visually — and AI is just one helpful feature inside it. We're highlighting it separately because thousands of teachers already use it daily, and the free Teacher account is one of the best deals in education software.

🎨 What Is Canva?

Canva is an online design tool used by tens of millions of teachers, students, and businesses. Think of it like an easier, friendlier version of PowerPoint or Photoshop — but instead of starting from a blank page, you start from thousands of pre-made templates built by professional designers. You just drag, drop, type, and recolor.

In simple terms:
  • You pick a template (a poster, worksheet, slideshow, anything)
  • Replace the example text with your own
  • Swap out images, colors, and fonts in two clicks
  • Download as PDF, PNG, or PowerPoint — ready to share or print

🛠️ Step-by-Step: Make Your First Canva Design

Let's build a simple welcome poster from scratch. Once you've done this once, every other design works the same way.

  1. Go to canva.com/education in your browser and create a free Teacher account (verify with your school email). If you already have an account, sign in.
  2. From the home page, type what you want in the search bar at the top — e.g., "classroom welcome poster", "reading rules anchor chart", or "math worksheet". Templates appear instantly.
  3. Left-click a template you like. It opens in the design editor — a screen with your template in the middle and a toolbar on the left.
  4. Change the text: double-click any text on the design and type your own words. The font, size, and color stay the same so the design still looks good.
  5. Change the colors: left-click any colored shape, then left-click the colored square in the top toolbar to pick a new color. Try matching your school colors.
  6. Change the images: right-click an image and choose Replace, or drag a new photo from the left sidebar onto it. The new image snaps right into place.
  7. Add new elements: the left sidebar has tabs for Elements (shapes, lines, stickers), Text, Uploads (your own images), and Photos. Left-click any one to drag it onto your design.
  8. Download when you're done: top-right corner, left-click Share → Download. Pick PDF for printing, PNG for sharing online, or PowerPoint if you need to edit it elsewhere.
💡 The big secret: You almost never start from scratch in Canva. You search for what you need, find a template that's 80% done, and just swap in your content. A polished poster takes 3 minutes instead of 30.

🤖 The AI Features Inside Canva

Canva has built-in AI tools too — they're called Magic Studio. Here's what's most useful for teachers:

  • Magic Write — generates text for your designs (lesson titles, classroom rules, prompts). Like a mini ChatGPT inside Canva.
  • Magic Edit — left-click any part of an image and describe what to change (e.g., "make the sky purple" or "add a chalkboard").
  • Background Remover — one click to cut out the background from any photo. Perfect for making sticker-style images of objects or student work.
  • Text to Image — generate brand-new images from a description, just like you learned in Steps 1–5. Use this for custom worksheet illustrations.
  • Magic Resize — turn a poster into a social media post, or a slideshow into a worksheet, with one click.

⚖️ Canva vs. AI Chat Tools — When to Use Which

Use Canva when… Use AI chat (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) when…
You want something that looks polished and professional You need to generate text content (quizzes, lesson plans, rubrics)
You're making posters, handouts, or anything visual You want to brainstorm or get ideas in plain English
You want to start from a template and customize You want to start from scratch with your own ideas
You need printable PDFs that look great every time You need flexible content that can become anything

Pro move: many teachers use both together — generate the content in ChatGPT or Claude, then drop it into a beautiful Canva template for a polished final product.

✅ Bottom line: Canva isn't part of the regular AI lessons because it works differently — but it's one of the most valuable free tools in education. Set up your free Teacher account once and you'll use it for the rest of your career.
